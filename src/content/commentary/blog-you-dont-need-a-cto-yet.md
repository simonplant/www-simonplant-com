There's a conversation I have with founders and CEOs more often than any other. It usually starts with some version of this:

*"We've gotten to $5M in revenue and now everything feels held together with duct tape. We think we need a CTO. Maybe. We're not sure what we actually need."*

That uncertainty is useful. Most companies in that position don't actually need a full-time CTO — at least not yet. But they do need something real, and "we'll figure it out" isn't it.

Here's what I've learned about this, after building teams from scratch at AWS, running a startup from zero to acquisition in 14 months, and spending the last two years as a fractional CTO for companies at exactly this inflection point.

---

## The false binary

When a company reaches the threshold where technical decisions are becoming expensive, the instinct is to frame it as a hiring question: *do we need a CTO or not?*

That's the wrong frame.

It conflates two things that are different enough to be dangerous: **technical execution** and **technology leadership**.

Technical execution is the work — building features, managing systems, shipping software, keeping the lights on. A strong engineering manager or a good VP of Engineering handles this. It's about throughput, quality, and team health.

Technology leadership is the judgment layer — which bets to make, how to structure systems for the next three years and not just the next three months, how to evaluate a vendor claim or an architecture decision, what "AI-enabled" actually means for your business vs. how a sales rep means it. It's about trajectory, not throughput.

Most growing companies have someone responsible for the first thing. They have nobody responsible for the second. And when technology becomes strategic — which it always does, sooner than expected — the absence of that judgment layer creates expensive problems that are hard to see clearly from inside the company.

---

## The timing trap

There's a specific failure mode I see repeatedly. A company scales without real technology leadership for longer than it should — usually because the founders have a technically capable person on staff and assume that's close enough. They're building product, shipping things, keeping customers happy. The lack of a strategic layer isn't visibly hurting them.

Then something breaks. The system can't handle the load. The platform debt has compounded to the point where new features take three times as long as they should. A security incident surfaces architecture decisions made under pressure two years ago. A key engineer leaves and nobody else fully understands how a critical component works.

At that point, the decision to hire a CTO gets made under duress — which is the worst time to make any important hire. The job spec is written around the immediate crisis. The interview process is compressed. The bias is toward whoever seems most confident about fixing the specific problem in front of you, rather than the person most capable of preventing the next five problems you haven't seen yet.

The companies that get this right don't wait for a crisis. They recognize the inflection point earlier — usually somewhere between $3M and $15M in revenue, often coinciding with a significant platform expansion, a fundraise, a new enterprise customer, or a competitive threat that's technology-shaped — and they get serious about technology leadership before the duct tape fails.

---

## What fractional actually means

The fractional model exists to solve a specific mismatch: the need for strategic technology leadership and the economic reality that most companies at this stage can't justify — or shouldn't commit to — a full-time executive compensation package for a role they're not sure they're ready for.

"Fractional CTO" has gotten muddied, though. Some people use it to mean "a consultant who helps you hire a real CTO." Some use it as a polite description for an embedded contractor who writes code. Neither of those is the model I think produces value.

What works: a fractional CTO functions as an actual executive, with real accountability, across a limited number of engagements. They attend your leadership team meetings. They have an opinion on your product roadmap and aren't shy about it. They've had hard conversations with engineers, vendors, and boards — and they'll have them with you. They're not just available for questions; they're driving a specific agenda.

That last part matters more than people realize. A fractional CTO who's purely reactive — answering questions when asked, reviewing things when invited — will produce a fraction of the value of one who shows up with a point of view and pushes on the things that need pushing on.

The model works well for companies in a few specific situations:

- **Scaling past the "everyone knows everything" phase** — when the informal technical knowledge that got you here is no longer sufficient for the decisions you're now making
- **Navigating a platform decision** — cloud migrations, major infrastructure investments, choosing between off-the-shelf and custom-built, figuring out what "AI-native" means for your architecture before committing
- **Evaluating technical leadership candidates** — when you know you'll eventually hire a full-time CTO and you need someone who can actually evaluate whether candidates are as good as they sound
- **Surviving an incident** — when something broke and you need to understand what happened, what it means, and what to actually do about it

And critically: a fractional engagement should have a defined scope and a clear outcome. The goal isn't to become permanently dependent on an outside advisor. It's to get the company through an inflection point in better shape than it would have managed otherwise.

## The AI problem is accelerating all of this

Here's the thing that's changed in the last 18 months: AI has compressed the timelines.

A few years ago, you could probably push the "we need serious technology leadership" decision to $20M in revenue without catastrophic consequences. The pace of change was slower. The technology bets were more constrained. The gap between a technically capable generalist and a deep specialist was smaller.

Now, every company — not just tech companies — is trying to figure out how to use AI tools to move faster, reduce costs, and not fall behind competitors who are experimenting aggressively. The decisions being made right now, in 2026, will determine competitive positions in ways that will be very hard to reverse.

And most of those decisions are being made by people who are smart and well-intentioned but don't have the background to tell a real AI capability from a vendor demo that looks impressive and falls apart in production. The difference between a useful AI integration and an expensive distraction that burns six months of engineering time is often just informed judgment at the right moment.

This is exactly the gap fractional technology leadership fills well. Not because fractional CTOs are magic — but because having someone with relevant experience in the room when these decisions are being made is cheap compared to the cost of getting them wrong.

## What to look for

If you decide to engage a fractional CTO — whether me or anyone else — a few things matter more than resume length.

**Real operator experience, not just advisory experience.** The most useful fractional CTOs have built teams, shipped software, managed systems that broke, made architectural decisions they later had to live with. Credentials and consulting background are fine context, but they don't substitute for having been accountable for outcomes.

**They should be direct.** The value of bringing in external leadership is precisely that they're not embedded in your organizational politics. If a fractional CTO is telling you primarily what you want to hear, you're paying for comfort, not judgment.

**They should be honest about fit.** Not every company needs a fractional CTO. Not every fractional CTO is right for every company. A good one will tell you if the engagement isn't the right structure for what you actually need, or if they're not the right person for your specific situation.

**Fewer engagements at once.** A fractional CTO running 10 clients isn't giving any of them meaningful time. The model only works if you're one of a small number of serious commitments — not a name on a client roster.
